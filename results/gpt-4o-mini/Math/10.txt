To compute \(\mathbf{a} \cdot (\mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{b})\), we start by recalling a property of the dot product and the cross product. Specifically, for any vectors \(\mathbf{u}\) and \(\mathbf{v}\), the dot product \(\mathbf{u} \cdot (\mathbf{u} \times \mathbf{v})\) is always zero. This is because the vector \(\mathbf{u} \times \mathbf{v}\) is orthogonal to \(\mathbf{u}\).

\[
\mathbf{a} = \begin{pmatrix} 4 \\ \sqrt{\pi} \\ -14 \end{pmatrix}, \quad \mathbf{b} = \begin{pmatrix} -1 \\ 3 \\ 10 \end{pmatrix}.

Thus, we can directly apply this property:

\[
\mathbf{a} \cdot (\mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{b}) = 0.
