To find the length \( AD \) in triangle \( ABC \) where \( \angle A = 90^\circ \), \( AC = 1 \), and \( AB = 5 \), and point \( D \) lies on ray \( \overrightarrow{AC} \) such that \( \angle DBC = 2 \angle CBA \), we will follow these steps:

### Step 1: Find the Length \( BC \)

Using the Pythagorean theorem in triangle \( ABC \):

\[
AB^2 = AC^2 + BC^2
\]

Substituting the known values:

\[
5^2 = 1^2 + BC^2
\]
\[
25 = 1 + BC^2
\]
\[
BC^2 = 24
\]
\[
BC = \sqrt{24} = 2\sqrt{6}
\]

### Step 2: Determine the Angles in Triangle \( ABC \)

Since \( \angle A = 90^\circ \), we can find the other angles using the tangent function:

\[
\tan(\angle CBA) = \frac{AC}{BC} = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{6}}
\]

Let \( \angle CBA = \theta \). Then:

\[
\tan(\theta) = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{6}}
\]

Using the relationship given in the problem, we have:

\[
\angle DBC = 2\angle CBA = 2\theta
\]

### Step 3: Use the Law of Sines in Triangle \( DBC \)

In triangle \( DBC \), we can apply the Law of Sines:

\[
\frac{DB}{\sin(\angle DBC)} = \frac{BC}{\sin(\angle BDC)}
\]

Let \( DB = x \). Then:

\[
\frac{x}{\sin(2\theta)} = \frac{2\sqrt{6}}{\sin(\angle BDC)}
\]

Using the double angle identity for sine:

\[
\sin(2\theta) = 2\sin(\theta)\cos(\theta)
\]

Thus, we can rewrite the equation as:

\[
\frac{x}{2\sin(\theta)\cos(\theta)} = \frac{2\sqrt{6}}{\sin(\angle BDC)}
\]

### Step 4: Find \( AD \)

Since point \( D \) lies on ray \( \overrightarrow{AC} \), we can express \( AD \) as:

\[
AD = AC + CD
\]

To find \( CD \), we need to express \( CD \) in terms of \( x \) and the angles. Since \( D \) is on ray \( AC \), we can use the sine rule again to find \( CD \).

However, we can also use the relationship between the angles. Since \( \angle DBC = 2\theta \), we can find \( \angle BDC \) using the triangle angle sum property:

\[
\angle BDC = 180^\circ - \angle DBC - \angle CBA = 180^\circ - 2\theta - \theta = 180^\circ - 3\theta
\]

### Step 5: Solve for \( AD \)

Using the sine rule in triangle \( ABC \):

\[
\frac{AC}{\sin(\angle ABC)} = \frac{AB}{\sin(\angle ACB)}
\]

We can find the lengths and angles needed to compute \( AD \).
