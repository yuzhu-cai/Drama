To convert the point \((1, -1, -6)\) from rectangular coordinates to cylindrical coordinates, we will use the results obtained from the previous calculations.

### Step 1: Calculate \(r\)

Using the formula for radial distance:

\[
r = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}
\]

Substituting \(x = 1\) and \(y = -1\):

\[
r = \sqrt{(1)^2 + (-1)^2} = \sqrt{1 + 1} = \sqrt{2}
\]

### Step 2: Calculate \(\theta\)

Using the formula for the angle:

\[
\theta = \text{atan2}(y, x)
\]

Substituting \(y = -1\) and \(x = 1\):

\[
\theta = \text{atan2}(-1, 1)
\]

Since the point \((1, -1)\) is in the fourth quadrant, we find:

\[
\theta = -\frac{\pi}{4}
\]

To express \(\theta\) in the range \(0 \leq \theta < 2\pi\), we can add \(2\pi\):

\[
\theta = -\frac{\pi}{4} + 2\pi = \frac{7\pi}{4}
\]

### Step 3: Identify \(z\)

The \(z\)-coordinate remains the same in cylindrical coordinates:

\[
z = -6
\]

### Final Result

Combining all the results, we express the point in cylindrical coordinates as:

\[
(r, \theta, z) = \left(\sqrt{2}, \frac{7\pi}{4}, -6\right)
\]

Thus, the point \((1, -1, -6)\) in rectangular coordinates is represented in cylindrical coordinates as:

\[
\boxed{\left(\sqrt{2}, \frac{7\pi}{4}, -6\right)}
\]
